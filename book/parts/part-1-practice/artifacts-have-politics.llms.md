## Purpose

The rest of this handbook is a *how-to*. It teaches you how to ask questions, read documentation, debug code, manage environments, move files, and ship projects. Those are the tools of the trade. This chapter asks a different kind of question: *whose* trade is it, *whose* values are baked into those tools, and *whose* problems have they been shaped around? Computing is not a neutral pipe that delivers whatever you pour into it. Every piece of software you use — every convention, every default, every permitted and forbidden operation — was built by specific people, funded by specific institutions, and constrained by specific laws. Knowing the history of those choices is part of being a competent paratechnical practitioner.

You will not become a better debugger by reading this chapter. You will, however, become a better citizen of the stack: someone who can explain why Unix permissions look the way they do, why the web has `robots.txt` but not a global opt-out of training data, why your favorite open-source library is maintained by two exhausted volunteers in Europe, and why “just use the default” is never really a neutral choice. The goal is not to make you cynical about computing. The goal is to recognize your position as a participant in a very long, very contested, still-unfolding story.

The title comes from Langdon Winner’s 1980 essay “Do Artifacts Have Politics?” ([Winner 1980](#ref-winner1980artifacts)), which argued that technologies embed political choices — sometimes deliberately, sometimes by accident — and that users, builders, and policymakers cannot reason about technology honestly without naming those choices. Forty-five years later, the argument is if anything more urgent, because the artifacts in question now mediate the majority of public life.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

2.  Identify at least three competing value systems that have shaped computing (national-security, monopoly-commercial, and hacker-counterculture) and give a concrete example of each.
3.  Trace the gender history of computing from the female “computers” of the 1940s to the underrepresentation patterns of today, and name at least three women whose contributions were minimized or erased.
4.  Explain how race and ethnicity have been embedded in computing systems via training data, proxy variables, and deployment contexts, with a specific example.
5.  Describe how computing built for a US / English-speaking / high-bandwidth context fails users elsewhere, and name at least two mechanisms (training data, language coverage, infrastructure assumptions).
6.  Summarize how modern states use computing for censorship, surveillance, and social control, and distinguish authoritarian from democratic uses (or explain why the distinction is harder than it looks).
7.  Summarize, in one paragraph each, the practical significance of the CFAA, Section 230, the DMCA, and GDPR for an ordinary developer.
8.  Apply a short checklist to a new technology before adopting, advocating for, or building it.

## Running theme: technology is never neutral

Whenever someone tells you “the technology is just a tool,” ask who built it, who paid for it, who gets to change it, who is measured by it, and who pays the costs when it fails. *Those* are the politics of the artifact. The tool will not tell you the answers; you have to go looking.

## 8.1 The Winner thesis: how artifacts have politics

Langdon Winner’s short 1980 essay is the starting point for almost every serious conversation about the politics of technology ([Winner 1980](#ref-winner1980artifacts)). Winner distinguished two ways a technology can be “political.” The first is *by design*: a system can be built to produce, reinforce, or constrain a particular social arrangement. His famous example was a set of parkway overpasses on Long Island, built under Robert Moses in the 1920s and 1930s, whose clearances were too low for public buses to pass underneath. The effect — possibly intentional, certainly durable — was that the beaches at the end of those parkways were easier to reach by private car than by bus, and therefore easier to reach by the car-owning middle class than by the poorer, disproportionately Black and Puerto Rican riders who depended on public transit. The bridges “had politics” not because they were made of political material but because their physical shape performed a social sorting function that a policy memo might have had trouble getting passed.

The second way is *inherently*: some technologies, Winner argued, strongly tend toward one social arrangement or another regardless of who deploys them. His canonical example was nuclear power, which he read as pushing toward centralized, hierarchical, security-state forms of governance because the fuel is hazardous, the plants are few and large, and the facilities must be defended. A solar panel on a roof, by contrast, is compatible with more distributed forms of organization. Winner was careful not to claim that technologies determine society outright; he claimed that they load the dice.

You can apply both readings to software. Proprietary operating systems with locked-down updates “have politics” the way the Moses overpasses did: someone decided which applications you may install, which peripherals will work, and which modifications void your warranty, and that decision is now literally built into the machine you are holding. Peer-to-peer file-sharing protocols “have politics” the Winner-inherent way: you can build a centralized moderation layer on top of BitTorrent, but the underlying topology keeps pulling back toward distribution, which is why the history of file-sharing law is a long series of attempts to re-centralize what the protocol wanted to scatter. Large language models “have politics” in both senses at once: their training corpora encode specific linguistic and cultural defaults (design), and their economics — the scale of compute required to train a frontier model — push strongly toward a small number of very large deployers (inherent). The exercises at the end of this chapter ask you to practice this kind of reading on the tools you use every day.

Winner’s essay is not the only framing. Science and technology studies (STS) scholars have extended it in many directions: Bowker and Star’s *Sorting Things Out* ([Bowker and Star 1999](#ref-bowker1999sorting)) traces how the most invisible technical objects — classification systems, medical records, racial categories — quietly decide who counts as what. MacKenzie’s *Inventing Accuracy* ([MacKenzie 1990](#ref-mackenzie1990inventing)) shows how nuclear missile guidance “accuracy” was not a neutral number but a politically constructed one. But Winner is where most non-specialists start, and his question — *what does this artifact do besides its stated function?* — is the one you should keep in your back pocket.

## 8.2 Cold War roots: from SAGE to ARPANET and the surveillance state

It is hard to overstate how much of modern computing grew directly out of the United States Cold War military budget. The first large digital computers in the US were built under wartime contracts: the ENIAC, completed in 1946, was commissioned by the Army’s Ballistic Research Laboratory to compute artillery firing tables. After the war, the money did not go away; it changed labels. Paul Edwards’s *The Closed World* ([Edwards 1996](#ref-edwards1996closed)) is the standard account of how Cold War anxiety about bombers, missiles, and nuclear command-and-control created a decades-long flood of funding for digital computing, real-time operating systems, networking, graphics, and human-computer interaction. Most of the concepts you take for granted — interactive computing, the mouse, the graphical interface, the idea of a “computer network” at all — have a SAGE, ARPA, or RAND ancestor in their family tree.

**SAGE** (Semi-Automatic Ground Environment, deployed by the US Air Force starting in the late 1950s) was a radar-and-computer system designed to detect incoming Soviet bombers and coordinate interceptor response. It was obsolete almost before it was finished — intercontinental ballistic missiles made bomber defense less relevant — but as a training ground for programmers, hardware engineers, software project managers, and systems thinkers, SAGE mattered enormously. Many of the people who built the commercial computing industry in the 1960s and 1970s had SAGE on their résumés.

**ARPA** (later DARPA, the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency) funded, among many other things, the ARPANET, the ancestor of the internet. The ARPANET was not built to survive a nuclear strike — that is a persistent myth — but it was built with military communication resilience in mind, and it was funded because the US Department of Defense wanted resource-sharing across expensive academic research computers it was already paying for. TCP/IP, DNS, email, the file-transfer protocol, and most of the other protocol-layer technology you use was developed under ARPA money or by people whose salaries traced back to it. That provenance is not inherently sinister — plenty of public goods have come out of the defense budget — but it shaped what kinds of problems got attention and what kinds did not. “Resilient packet delivery between trusted institutional peers” got excellent engineering; “how do we authenticate billions of strangers on a hostile network” got almost none, which is why the web had to bolt on cryptography, identity, and trust decades later and is still not done.

The surveillance half of the story is harder to separate from the rest than most textbooks pretend. The National Security Agency (NSA) is a founding customer of cryptography research, a major funder of high-performance computing, and the source of many technical standards, sometimes with consequences the public learned about only later — for example, the 2013 Snowden disclosures showed that the NSA had been systematically tapping internet backbone traffic, collecting metadata on a population scale, and in at least one documented case nudging a commercial cryptographic standard toward a design that made it easier to break. *Black Code* ([Deibert 2013](#ref-deibert2013blackcode)) gives the broader global picture of state surveillance capabilities, and Zuboff’s *Surveillance Capitalism* ([Zuboff 2019](#ref-zuboff2019surveillance)) argues that the commercial internet then learned from and extended the state’s playbook. The point is not that every computing technology is secretly military, but that the *shape* of computing — what is easy, what is hard, what is default, what is exotic — carries a Cold War signature that is still legible today if you know to look for it.

## 8.3 Monopoly, centralization, and antitrust

Once you understand that computing came out of military spending, the second thing to understand is that it almost immediately became one of the most concentrated industries in the history of capitalism. There is a pattern: a dominant firm emerges, regulators eventually notice, an antitrust case drags on for years, a consent decree reshapes the industry, and a new dominant firm emerges in the gap. This is not an accident of personality or of any particular era’s villains. It is the pattern you would expect in an industry with huge fixed costs, near-zero marginal costs, strong network effects, and few regulatory guardrails.

**AT&T and Bell Labs.** For most of the twentieth century, American telephony was run by a single regulated monopoly, American Telephone & Telegraph. That monopoly funded Bell Labs, which produced the transistor, information theory, the C programming language, and Unix — arguably the single most generative research lab in the history of computing. It also, under a 1956 antitrust consent decree, was forbidden to enter the computer business, which is why Unix was initially licensed cheaply to universities and became the genetic material for most modern operating systems. AT&T itself was broken up in 1982 into the “Baby Bells” and a smaller AT&T. Much of the long-distance and regional telecom infrastructure that the modern internet runs on top of is still the shattered inheritance of that monopoly.

**IBM.** Through the 1960s and 1970s, IBM was so dominant in mainframes that “computing” and “IBM” were near-synonyms in the business press. The US Department of Justice filed an antitrust case against IBM in 1969 and pursued it until 1982, when the case was withdrawn without judgment. IBM’s response to the antitrust pressure — unbundling software from hardware sales — is one of the reasons a commercial software industry exists at all. IBM also decided, during the design of the original PC in 1980-81, to source the operating system from an outside vendor rather than build it in-house, which is how a small Seattle company called Microsoft came to own the dominant PC operating system franchise.

**Microsoft.** The 1990s version of the same pattern: Microsoft used its Windows monopoly to push Internet Explorer against Netscape, the US Department of Justice filed antitrust suits, a judge ordered the company broken up in 2000, the order was overturned on appeal, and a consent decree followed. By the time the legal dust settled, the browser war was over and the locus of competition had already moved to search, which Google was in the process of winning, and then to mobile, which Apple and Google split.

**The current round.** Today’s dominant firms — often abbreviated FAANG, GAFAM, or “Big Tech” — are under antitrust scrutiny in the US, the EU, and several Asian jurisdictions. The Digital Markets Act, passed by the EU in 2022, is the first serious attempt to regulate “gatekeeper” platforms directly rather than case by case. The outcome of the current round is unwritten as of this book’s publication, but the pattern is clear: dominant computing firms accumulate power faster than regulators can respond, the remediation (when it comes) is partial and slow, and the firms that benefit most from the remediation are often the ones that become dominant in the next round. The politics of the artifact here are about *who gets to decide*: when “the platform” makes a rule, whether it is “no guns in marketplace listings” or “apps must use our payment processor” or “no training models on our public API without a license,” that rule has roughly the force of law for the hundreds of millions of people who depend on the platform, but it is made without any of the democratic accountability we expect of laws.

Zuboff’s argument in *Surveillance Capitalism* ([Zuboff 2019](#ref-zuboff2019surveillance)) is that the underlying business model — collect behavioral data at scale, sell predictions about what users will do next — is itself a political choice, not a technical necessity. Couldry and Mejias ([Couldry and Mejias 2019](#ref-couldry2019costs)) call this “data colonialism” and argue it repeats the extractive logic of nineteenth-century resource extraction with personal data as the resource. You do not have to accept either framing in full to notice that the defaults of the commercial internet — tracking on by default, consent buried in click-through agreements, identifiers that follow you across sites and apps — were chosen, and could have been chosen differently.

## 8.4 The hacker counterculture and the free software movement

Alongside the military-funded and the commercial-monopoly stories, a third tradition has always been running: the hacker counterculture, and its institutional heir, the free and open source software movement. Steven Levy’s *Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution* ([Levy 1984](#ref-levy1984hackers)) popularized the term “hacker ethic” — roughly: information should be free, authority should be mistrusted, access to computers should be unlimited, judge people by what they can do rather than by credentials. The ethic emerged in the student labs at MIT in the 1960s, traveled west with the Homebrew Computer Club in the 1970s (where Steve Wozniak and a teenage Steve Jobs were regulars), and shaped the culture of the early personal-computer industry and, later, the public internet.

Fred Turner’s *From Counterculture to Cyberculture* ([Turner 2006](#ref-turner2006counterculture)) tells a more uncomfortable version of the story: that the libertarian, individualist, anti-institutional strand of 1960s counterculture — the strand that passed through Stewart Brand’s *Whole Earth Catalog* and the *Whole Earth ’Lectronic Link* — merged with Cold War systems thinking to produce a particular vision of networked computing as inherently liberatory. That vision shaped the rhetoric of the commercial web of the 1990s (“cyberspace,” “information wants to be free,” “the net interprets censorship as damage and routes around it”), and it was partly right and partly wildly optimistic about how power would actually redistribute once everyone was online.

The institutional heir of the hacker ethic is **free software**, organized around Richard Stallman’s Free Software Foundation and the GNU project (started 1983), whose moral claim is that software users should have four freedoms: to run a program for any purpose, to study and modify it, to redistribute it, and to redistribute modifications. The legal instrument of that claim is the GNU General Public License (GPL), a “copyleft” license that uses copyright law against itself — you may use and modify GPL-licensed code, but any derivative work you distribute must also be GPL-licensed, which propagates the freedoms. The **Linux kernel**, started by Linus Torvalds in 1991, combined with the GNU userland tools, produced the first complete free Unix-like operating system, and today Linux runs most of the public internet, most of the world’s smartphones (via Android), and most high-performance computing.

The **open source** movement, launched in the late 1990s, reframed the free software argument in more business-friendly terms: the case for open development as a superior *engineering* practice, not necessarily a moral one. This rebrand lowered the barrier for corporate participation and is one reason large chunks of modern infrastructure — Apache, MySQL, PostgreSQL, nginx, Kubernetes, Python, most of NumPy and pandas, and the rest of the data-science stack — are open source today. It also, critics argue, diluted the movement’s ethical claims and created the current ecosystem in which most of the world’s critical infrastructure is maintained by a handful of unpaid volunteers whose burnout is a recurring security crisis (the 2014 Heartbleed vulnerability in OpenSSL, the 2021 Log4Shell vulnerability in Log4j, and the 2024 XZ Utils backdoor all surfaced this pattern). The 2024 XZ Utils case is particularly worth reading about: a patient social-engineering attack over several years by a single maintainer account nearly slipped a hidden backdoor into the compression library that ships with essentially every Linux distribution. It was caught by one Microsoft engineer who noticed his SSH logins were running half a second slow.

The values of these three traditions — the national-security tradition, the commercial-monopoly tradition, and the hacker-counterculture tradition — are not fully compatible, and every serious fight about the shape of computing is downstream of which tradition is winning. Should operating systems be locked down (security) or open (hacker ethic)? Should platforms be allowed to moderate speech on their own terms (commercial) or forced to host content they find repugnant (competing versions of freedom)? Should cryptography be strong enough to thwart state surveillance (hacker), or backdoored for lawful access (security), or watered down for export (commercial)? None of these are purely technical questions, and you cannot reason about the artifacts without reasoning about the values they encode.

## 8.5 Who counted? Gender in the history of computing

If you learned computing history from a standard textbook, you probably learned it as a procession of men: Turing, von Neumann, Wozniak, Jobs, Gates, Torvalds, Page, Brin, Zuckerberg, Musk. That procession is historically false in a specific and instructive way: for the first fifty years of the field, programming was a low-status, majority-female profession, and the current gender composition of computing is the result of a deliberate and documentable *reversal*, not an unchanged baseline.

Start with the word “computer.” Until the mid-twentieth century, a “computer” was a person, usually a woman, who performed numerical calculations by hand — ballistic trajectories, astronomical tables, engineering calculations. The Harvard College Observatory in the late 1800s employed dozens of women as “computers” to classify stars and compute their positions; this group included Henrietta Swan Leavitt, whose work on Cepheid variable stars made Edwin Hubble’s later measurement of the expansion of the universe possible. When the ENIAC, the first general-purpose electronic digital computer, went into service in 1946, the six people hired to program it — to physically patch cables and set thousand-position switches so the machine could compute a new problem — were all women: Kathleen McNulty, Frances Bilas, Betty Jean Jennings, Ruth Lichterman, Marlyn Wescoff, and Frances Snyder. Their work was considered clerical and was uncredited in the 1946 press coverage, which celebrated the machine and the hardware men who built it. The ENIAC women’s contributions were largely recovered only in the 1980s, through the historian Kathryn Kleiman and the documentary *Top Secret Rosies*.

**Ada Lovelace** (1815-1852), writing a century earlier, is often cited as the first computer programmer for her notes on Charles Babbage’s unbuilt Analytical Engine, in which she described an algorithm for computing Bernoulli numbers. Her framing of the machine as capable of manipulating symbols (not just numbers) is startlingly modern. **Grace Hopper** (1906-1992), a US Navy officer and computer scientist, led the team that built the first compiler (A-0, 1952), and was the driving force behind COBOL, the first widely-used high-level programming language for business computing. Hopper popularized the term “bug” and “debugging” after a literal moth was pulled from a relay in the Mark II computer in 1947. **Kathleen Booth** co-designed one of the first functioning stored-program computers (ARC, 1948) and wrote the first assembly language. **Dorothy Vaughan**, **Mary Jackson**, and **Katherine Johnson** — the Black women mathematicians at NASA Langley whose story was popularized by Margot Lee Shetterly’s *Hidden Figures* (2016) — did the calculations that put John Glenn in orbit and Apollo 11 on the moon; Vaughan retrained herself and her “West Area Computing” unit in FORTRAN when NASA transitioned to electronic computers, making them the first generation of Black women software programmers in the US space program. These names are a small sample. You can find many more in Nathan Ensmenger’s *The Computer Boys Take Over* ([Ensmenger 2010](#ref-ensmenger2010computerboys)) and Mar Hicks’s *Programmed Inequality* ([Hicks 2017](#ref-hicks2017programmed)).

The reversal from female-majority to male-majority programming happened roughly between 1960 and 1985 and has several well-documented causes. Ensmenger argues that as programming became economically important, professional societies, aptitude tests, and university computer-science departments adopted credentialing practices (IQ-style “programmer aptitude” tests, requirements for mathematics degrees, apprenticeship networks) that systematically advantaged men. Hicks’s account focuses on the United Kingdom: the British civil service had a majority-female computing workforce in the 1940s and 1950s, and Hicks shows how government policy deliberately drove women out of the field in the 1960s in the name of “professionalizing” it, resulting in a catastrophic loss of skilled labor that contributed to the collapse of the British computing industry. In the United States, the percentage of computer-science bachelor’s degrees awarded to women peaked near 37% in 1984 and has been lower ever since, dropping to about 18% in the late 2000s before recovering slightly.

The modern underrepresentation pattern is not limited to gender composition. It shows up in pay gaps, in attrition from industry (women leave computing jobs at significantly higher rates than men, especially in mid-career), in harassment and hostile-work-environment cases, and in the demographics of venture-capital funding. The 2014 “Gamergate” episode — a sustained harassment campaign against women game developers, critics, and journalists, coordinated through messaging boards and Twitter — is frequently cited as a turning point in how the broader public understood online harassment as a patterned phenomenon rather than a collection of isolated incidents. Zeynep Tufekci’s *Twitter and Tear Gas* ([Tufekci 2017](#ref-tufekci2017twitter)) is a good general guide to how networked platforms shape who gets to speak and who gets shouted down, though it is not specifically about gender.

*Data Feminism* ([D’Ignazio and Klein 2020](#ref-dignazio2020data)) offers one framework for thinking about the current moment: it asks who is counted in datasets, who is counting, and who benefits from the counting, and it treats the answers to those questions as inseparable from the techniques of data analysis you learned in the rest of this book. The statistical apparatus you use in pandas is not a neutral observer of a pre-existing world; it was shaped by who had the authority to design the surveys, the categories, and the analyses. That is not a reason to stop doing data work. It is a reason to ask, every time, *whose* data you are working with and whose questions it was built to answer.

## 8.6 Race, bias, and the reproduction of marginalization

Computing systems are often described as “objective” or “unbiased” in contrast to fallible human judgment. The empirical record does not support that claim. A long and growing body of evidence shows that computational systems regularly encode and amplify the racial, ethnic, and class biases of the societies that build them — sometimes because the training data reflects historical discrimination, sometimes because the problem was framed in a way that made bias inevitable, sometimes because the humans deploying the system pointed it at the people they were already policing. The technical vocabulary for this is “algorithmic bias” or “disparate impact”; the substantive argument is that the claim of objectivity is itself a political move that launders older forms of discrimination through new interfaces.

**Latanya Sweeney’s name-search study** (2013) was one of the early widely-cited empirical demonstrations. Sweeney, a computer scientist then at Harvard, noticed that Google search queries for “Black-identifying” first names (Latanya, DeShawn, Darnell) returned advertisements suggesting arrest records at significantly higher rates than queries for “white-identifying” first names (Brad, Jill, Kristen) — even controlling for whether any arrest record actually existed. The finding implicated not only Google’s ad auction but the advertiser (a service called Instant Checkmate) and the underlying ad-targeting feedback loops that rewarded click-through on alarming framings. Sweeney’s broader work on medical privacy — including her famous 1997 demonstration that she could re-identify the then-Governor of Massachusetts from a “de-identified” public hospital dataset — helped establish the field now called *privacy-preserving computation*.

**ProPublica’s 2016 “Machine Bias” investigation** ([Angwin et al. 2016](#ref-angwin2016machinebias)) analyzed COMPAS, a proprietary risk-assessment tool used in some US jurisdictions to estimate the probability that a criminal defendant would re-offend. ProPublica found that the tool’s false-positive rate (classifying a defendant as high-risk who did not re-offend) was roughly twice as high for Black defendants as for white defendants, while its false-negative rate was higher for white defendants. The vendor, Northpointe (now Equivant), disputed the analysis and argued that by a different definition of fairness — calibration — the tool was fair across groups. The resulting academic debate showed that the common notions of “fairness” in classification are mathematically incompatible for groups with different base rates: you cannot satisfy equal false-positive rates, equal false-negative rates, and equal calibration simultaneously unless the underlying base rates are equal. This is not a technical defect you can patch; it is a structural property of the problem that forces a *choice* about which kind of fairness you prioritize, and that choice is political, not technical. Eubanks’s *Automating Inequality* ([Eubanks 2017](#ref-eubanks2017automating)) documents similar dynamics in US welfare eligibility, child-protective-services risk assessment, and homelessness triage.

**Buolamwini and Gebru’s “Gender Shades” study** (2018) ([Buolamwini and Gebru 2018](#ref-buolamwini2018gendershades)) evaluated three commercial face-analysis APIs (from IBM, Microsoft, and Face++) and found that the systems worked best on lighter-skinned men and worst on darker-skinned women, with error rates differing by up to 34 percentage points across subgroups. The finding was hard to dismiss as a one-off because Buolamwini had constructed a deliberately balanced evaluation set by skin type and gender — a dataset that did not previously exist in any commercial benchmark, which was itself a clue that nobody in the industry had been measuring what she was measuring. Within a year of publication, the vendors had reduced the disparities substantially, which is the good news; the less-good news is that none of them were measuring the disparities before the paper came out. That gap — between “we do not measure this” and “we measure this and it is now a KPI” — is where accountability actually lives.

Safiya Noble’s *Algorithms of Oppression* ([Noble 2018](#ref-noble2018algorithms)) extends the argument to search engines: Noble documented how image-search and auto-complete results for “Black girls” and similar queries surfaced sexually explicit and dehumanizing content for years, and how those results reflected both the advertiser-driven economics of search and the historical absence of Black women in the positions that would have caught the patterns earlier. Ruha Benjamin’s *Race After Technology* ([Benjamin 2019](#ref-benjamin2019race)) coined the useful term “the New Jim Code” for the way that ostensibly neutral algorithmic systems reproduce older racial caste structures through proxies: ZIP code standing in for race, “neighborhood reputation” standing in for redlining, “risk scores” standing in for who the police already bother. Kate Crawford’s *Atlas of AI* ([Crawford 2021](#ref-crawford2021atlas)) broadens the lens again, arguing that you cannot understand modern AI without tracing where its training data, its compute, its minerals, and its labor come from.

The practical upshot for a working data-science student is sharp: if you train a classifier on historical arrest data, you have built a classifier that predicts *who gets arrested*, not *who commits crimes*, and you have laundered a pattern of historical policing into a model that the next generation of policing will cite as evidence. The phrase “garbage in, garbage out” understates the problem: it is not that the inputs are random noise, it is that the inputs carry structured information about *who counts*, and the model will preserve that structure unless you actively fight it. The fight is worth having, but it starts with refusing the claim that the system was ever neutral in the first place.

## 8.7 Beyond the West: global computing and digital colonialism

Most of the computing tools you will learn in this book were designed for a particular kind of user: an English-speaking (or at least Latin-alphabet-reading) adult with a high-speed internet connection, a modern device, reliable electricity, and a credit card or bank account. That profile describes a small fraction of the world’s population. The habits of computing you absorb in a US or European university classroom will fail — quietly or loudly — when you try to apply them to users, data, or infrastructure from the rest of the world. Some of those failures are technical. Most of them are political: choices about whose problems get solved first, whose languages get supported, whose laws get enforced, and whose data gets extracted.

**Language coverage.** Unicode now encodes more than 150,000 characters across more than 150 scripts, but the quality of support for those scripts in ordinary software varies by several orders of magnitude. English and a handful of European languages are well supported end-to-end. Mandarin, Japanese, Korean, Arabic, and Hindi are reasonably supported in mainstream software. Languages that use right-to-left scripts (Arabic, Hebrew, Persian, Urdu), complex combining marks (Devanagari, Tamil, Thai), or tonal diacritics (Vietnamese, Yoruba) still trip over layout bugs, font-rendering bugs, and search-index bugs in 2020s software. Languages that are spoken by millions of people but happen not to be official state languages — Hausa, Wolof, Quechua, Aymara, most indigenous languages of the Americas and Australia, most Nigerian languages other than English — are often effectively invisible to the machine-learning pipelines that will decide what “all languages” means for the next decade of software. Large language models are especially stark here: the fraction of their training data in any given language is approximately the fraction of the global internet that happens to be written in that language, which approximates the existing power distribution of the internet rather than the distribution of people who actually speak the language. A model that has read 200,000 Wikipedia articles in English and 400 in Wolof is not a “general-purpose” language model; it is an English model with a thin Wolof veneer, and it will confabulate more confidently and more harmfully in Wolof than in English.

**Infrastructure assumptions.** “Always online” is a US and European default, not a global one. Intermittent connectivity, expensive bandwidth, and small data caps are the norm for a majority of internet users. The conventions of modern web development — large JavaScript bundles, image-heavy pages, telemetry on every interaction, cloud-first architectures that fail closed when the cloud is unreachable — systematically disadvantage users on mobile data in places where mobile data is expensive. The “progressive web app” movement of the 2010s was in part a reaction to this, led by engineers at Google and elsewhere who had been working in India and sub-Saharan Africa and noticed that their Western-designed products were unusable for their target users. Facebook’s “Free Basics” / Internet.org program, launched in 2013, was another reaction — a walled garden of subsidized services that would be free to access on partner mobile networks in developing countries — but it was banned in India in 2016 after a sustained campaign arguing that it violated net-neutrality principles and privileged a US corporation’s view of what “the internet” should mean.

**Training data and the model of the world.** Modern machine-learning models are, among other things, compressed summaries of the corpora they were trained on. When those corpora are scraped from the English-speaking internet, the models learn an English-speaking-internet’s view of which cities exist, which medical symptoms are described in which words, what a “normal” family looks like, what a face looks like, what a street looks like, which scientific claims are credible, and which are fringe. Deploy such a model in Nairobi, Dhaka, or La Paz, and it will give confident answers about a world it has barely seen. This is not a conspiracy; it is the inherited structure of the available data. But it is also not neutral, and the responsible way to deploy such models outside their training distribution is to *measure* the failure, name it, and constrain the use accordingly.

**Data colonialism.** Couldry and Mejias’s term ([Couldry and Mejias 2019](#ref-couldry2019costs)) is a deliberate provocation, but the empirical pattern is real: datasets collected in the Global South (genomic data, agricultural imagery, behavioral data from low-income users of free apps, content moderated by workers in Kenya and the Philippines) are often processed, monetized, and turned into products by companies headquartered in the Global North, with the revenue accruing to the companies and almost none of it returning to the communities whose data was extracted. Regulatory regimes in the Global South are often weaker, making it legally and politically cheaper to run the data pipeline there and sell the model elsewhere. The One Laptop Per Child program of the mid-2000s, whose mission was to put a rugged low-cost laptop in the hands of every child in the developing world, is sometimes cited as the optimistic limit of this discussion (and was itself criticized for exporting a particular Western theory of “constructionist” education regardless of local pedagogical needs), and the Cambridge Analytica / Facebook scandal of 2016-18, whose tactics were rehearsed in Kenyan and Nigerian elections before being applied in the US and UK, is sometimes cited as the cynical limit.

The practical habit to develop is this: whenever you see the words “the user” or “users” or “everyone,” stop and ask which user, where, speaking what language, on what device, on what connection, under which legal regime. If the answer is “a college student in California on the campus wifi on a MacBook,” say so explicitly. That is not an insult to the work; it is honesty about its scope.

## 8.8 Digital authoritarianism, censorship, and surveillance

If computing has politics, states have noticed. Over the past two decades, a playbook has emerged for using networked computing to shore up state power — filtering the information citizens can reach, tracking the movements and communications of dissidents, automating identification and prosecution of protesters, and nudging public discourse through platform pressure and computational propaganda. This is usually called **digital authoritarianism**, and it is documented and tracked by organizations such as the Citizen Lab at the University of Toronto, Freedom House, and Access Now. Deibert’s *Black Code* ([Deibert 2013](#ref-deibert2013blackcode)) is a readable primer written by one of the Citizen Lab’s founders.

**The Great Firewall of China**, formally the Golden Shield Project, is the most thoroughly studied state-level filtering system in the world. Its technical components include DNS poisoning, IP blocking, deep packet inspection, keyword filtering, and TCP reset injection, operated at the border of the Chinese internet; its institutional components include the requirement that platforms operating inside China comply with content takedown requests quickly, and the coupling of those requirements to a real-name identity regime for users. The Firewall is not a single artifact; it is a shifting portfolio of techniques, and users inside China routinely route around individual filters (via VPNs and other tools), while the operators respond by escalating. The point of the Firewall is not to achieve perfect filtering — it is to make reaching foreign information costly and risky enough to move most users onto domestic platforms where moderation can be applied directly. In this it has largely succeeded. China’s 2017 Cybersecurity Law codified data-localization requirements for critical information infrastructure and gave state security services broad access to data stored inside the country; subsequent laws (the 2021 Data Security Law and the 2021 Personal Information Protection Law) extended the regime.

**Russia’s SORM** (System for Operational-Investigative Activities), operational in various forms since 1995, requires Russian telecom and internet providers to install equipment that gives the Federal Security Service (FSB) real-time access to their traffic. SORM is a particularly clean example of surveillance built into the network at the protocol level — it is a legal obligation on infrastructure operators, not an ad-hoc practice, and it has expanded through several generations (SORM-1, SORM-2, SORM-3) as new technologies arrived. Russia’s 2019 “sovereign internet” law extended the regime by requiring domestic routing for Russian traffic and giving the state authority to cut off external connectivity during “emergencies.”

**Iran** has used targeted internet shutdowns during protests — most notably during the November 2019 fuel-price protests, when the government cut internet access nationwide for several days — and has developed extensive infrastructure for filtering specific services (Twitter, Facebook, and many VPNs are blocked by default). Similar shutdown patterns have been documented in Belarus, Kazakhstan, Ethiopia, Myanmar, and India (which has led the world in subnational internet shutdowns for several years running, especially in Kashmir). Access Now’s \#KeepItOn project maintains a running log of internet shutdowns globally.

**Pegasus**, a commercial spyware product developed by the Israeli firm NSO Group, is the canonical example of the commercial surveillance industry. Pegasus can compromise a target’s phone remotely, often through zero-click exploits (no user interaction required), and exfiltrate messages, contacts, location, microphone, and camera. The Citizen Lab has documented Pegasus deployments against journalists, dissidents, lawyers, and diplomats in many countries. The commercial spyware industry’s existence is itself a political choice: the same expertise that finds and reports security vulnerabilities for defensive purposes can be (and is) sold to governments as offensive capability, and the regulatory regime that would distinguish one use from the other is weak in most jurisdictions.

**The Snowden disclosures** of 2013 showed that democratic governments, not just authoritarian ones, had built mass-surveillance capabilities that most of their voters did not know about. The documents leaked by NSA contractor Edward Snowden revealed programs that collected telephony metadata on most US calls (under the PATRIOT Act’s Section 215), tapped transatlantic fiber-optic cables in cooperation with UK intelligence (GCHQ’s Tempora program), and compromised the endpoint security of commercial products. The disclosures prompted a political backlash, some legal reforms (the 2015 USA Freedom Act narrowed the telephony metadata program in the US), and a large and durable shift in the technology industry toward default encryption: HTTPS coverage of the web went from roughly half of page loads in 2013 to more than 95% by 2023, and end-to-end encryption became a standard feature of major messaging apps. That shift is one of the clearer cases where “technology is political” cuts both ways: a state surveillance regime triggered a commercial re-engineering of default protections, which then constrained future surveillance.

The line between authoritarian and democratic uses of these capabilities is real but harder to draw than it looks from either side. Democratic states use face-recognition at protests; authoritarian states invoke public-safety justifications that would not be out of place in a Western city council meeting. The question to keep asking is not “is this country an authoritarian one” but “what is this specific capability, who has it, what oversight is there, and what happens when it is misused?” Tufekci’s *Twitter and Tear Gas* ([Tufekci 2017](#ref-tufekci2017twitter)) is useful here for the subtler point that network effects change the politics of protest themselves — it is easier than ever to organize a march, and harder than ever to sustain the institutions that turn a march into lasting change. Morozov’s *The Net Delusion* ([Morozov 2011](#ref-morozov2011net)) is the standard skeptical counter to the 2000s “liberation technology” rhetoric; the two books are worth reading together.

## 8.9 Labor, platforms, and algorithmic management

The shiny surface of a modern platform — the app, the website, the AI chatbot — sits on top of a great deal of human labor that is usually invisible and usually underpaid. Some of that labor is well-known: the drivers for Uber and Lyft, the riders for DoorDash and Deliveroo, the shoppers for Instacart. Some of it is much less visible: the people who label training data for machine-learning models, the content moderators who watch beheading videos for eight hours a day so that the rest of us do not have to, the warehouse workers whose pace is set by a handheld scanner that counts the seconds between picks. Mary Gray and Siddharth Suri’s *Ghost Work* ([Gray and Suri 2019](#ref-gray2019ghost)) is the canonical account of this labor and a good entry point to the literature.

**Gig work and algorithmic management.** A gig worker’s “employer” is, formally, an algorithm. The app tells the worker which jobs to take, which routes to drive, how long to spend at each stop, and when the day is over. The app rewards or punishes in ways that would be illegal from a human manager in many jurisdictions (instant deactivation, opaque scoring, no appeal). The legal fight over whether gig workers are employees or independent contractors — resolved differently in California, the UK, the EU, and elsewhere — is really a fight about which twentieth-century labor protections (minimum wage, overtime, unemployment insurance, workplace safety, collective bargaining) carry over into algorithmically managed work. Companies have a strong interest in the answer being “none of them.” Every time a gig-worker protection law passes, the platforms spend large sums campaigning against it, and the legal status of gig work has become one of the most expensive and most watched policy fights in the US and the EU.

**Content moderation as invisible labor.** When a platform removes a violent, sexual, or otherwise policy-violating post, the actual decision is usually made — directly or with AI assistance — by a human content moderator working for a subcontractor in the Philippines, Kenya, India, Poland, or Ireland. Moderators review thousands of traumatic items a day. Lawsuits in several jurisdictions (notably the 2020 Facebook settlement with US moderators, and the 2023 unfair-dismissal case brought by Kenyan moderators against Sama and Meta) have documented rates of post-traumatic stress disorder comparable to those of combat veterans. The labor that makes social media *usable* is almost entirely outsourced, hidden, and precarious. It is worth knowing this the next time someone claims that “AI will handle moderation”; as of the mid-2020s, “AI” largely means “a model that flags things for a human moderator to handle,” and the human moderator is almost always the low-wage contractor.

**Training-data labeling.** The same dynamic applies to the upstream work of building training data. Someone has to label the images, transcribe the audio, rank the model outputs for reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), and write the demonstration data for instruction-tuning. Most of that work is done by contractors for pennies per task; *Ghost Work* documents the workers, their working conditions, and the platforms (Amazon Mechanical Turk, Scale AI, Remotasks, and others) that coordinate them. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and other frontier-model developers rely heavily on these pipelines. The politics here are layered: the workers who make the AI possible are rarely credited, rarely well-compensated, and rarely in a position to refuse the work.

**Warehouse tracking and workplace surveillance.** Amazon warehouses are the best-documented example, but the pattern is common in logistics and fulfillment. Handheld scanners track the time between each “pick” and flag workers who fall below a rate. Bathroom breaks are timed. Productivity metrics are aggregated and used for automatic termination. From the worker’s perspective, the “algorithm” is a relentless, unappealable middle manager that never sleeps. From the company’s perspective, it is a productivity optimization that happens to be implemented in software. Both descriptions are true, which is exactly the point Winner was making: the artifact encodes the relationship.

**Open-source maintainer burnout and the unpaid-infrastructure problem.** In the opposite direction, the modern internet runs on software that is mostly unpaid. A small open-source library that is a dependency of your favorite framework, which is a dependency of a major service, which is a dependency of your bank, may be maintained by one person in their spare time. When that person burns out, retires, or has a political dispute with a downstream corporate user, the consequences propagate in ways the commercial users of the library rarely plan for. The 2014 Heartbleed vulnerability in OpenSSL, the 2016 “left-pad” withdrawal from npm that briefly broke a large fraction of the JavaScript ecosystem, the 2021 Log4Shell vulnerability in Log4j, and the 2024 XZ Utils backdoor are all manifestations of the same structural problem: the public depends on infrastructure that nobody, specifically, is responsible for funding. There are partial solutions (GitHub Sponsors, Open Collective, the Linux Foundation, corporate sponsorship programs), but none of them have solved the coordination problem at scale.

**Right-to-repair.** A related fight is about who is allowed to fix the devices they own. Manufacturers have used digital locks, proprietary screws, software-side “pairing” between components, and anti-circumvention laws to restrict third-party repair of phones, laptops, farm equipment (the John Deere cases are particularly famous), medical devices, and cars. Right-to-repair advocates argue that the consumer who bought the object should be allowed to fix it, or to hire an independent repair shop to fix it, without the manufacturer’s permission. Several US states and the EU have passed right-to-repair legislation in the 2020s, with varying scope. The politics here are about the afterlife of an artifact: who controls what happens to it after the sale.

The common thread across all of these is that the *apparent* automation is almost always supported by hidden human labor, usually cheaper and more precarious than the labor it replaced, and usually structured so that the benefits accrue to the platform and the costs (physical, psychological, financial) accrue to the workers and the discarded devices.

## 8.10 Environmental and material costs of computing

Computing feels immaterial when you are using it. The screen is light; the cloud is in the sky. The actual computer is sweating in a data center thousands of miles away, drinking electricity and water, and the chips inside it are made of rare and difficult materials that were mined, refined, and assembled by people whose working conditions you will not hear about unless you go looking. Crawford’s *Atlas of AI* ([Crawford 2021](#ref-crawford2021atlas)) is the best single book on this, and it makes the case that treating AI as “just software” obscures an enormous and growing physical footprint.

**Electricity.** Data centers already account for a meaningful fraction of global electricity use, and the training and inference cost of large language models has pushed that share upward sharply since 2020. The International Energy Agency has projected that data-center electricity demand will roughly double in the second half of the 2020s, driven primarily by AI workloads. A single training run for a frontier language model can consume gigawatt-hours of electricity; serving the resulting model to hundreds of millions of users consumes more. Some of this electricity comes from low-carbon sources (hydro, nuclear, wind, solar), and hyperscaler companies have invested heavily in power-purchase agreements for clean energy. Some of it comes from natural gas and, in a few cases, re-opened coal plants. The net effect on emissions depends on the grid where the data center sits, the emissions intensity of the next marginal kilowatt-hour that is added to meet the demand, and the counterfactual of what that electricity would otherwise have been used for. These are hard questions and the honest answer is that the sector’s claims deserve scrutiny.

**Water.** Data centers are also thirsty. Cooling large server halls requires either air conditioning (electricity-intensive) or evaporative cooling (water-intensive). Reports from the Phoenix and Dallas areas in the United States, and from parts of the Netherlands, Ireland, Chile, and Uruguay, have documented water-use disputes between data-center operators and local communities facing drought. A single large data center can use millions of gallons of water per day. As with electricity, the picture is mixed: some operators use recycled gray water, some use closed-loop cooling systems, some have moved toward air cooling in climates that support it. But “the cloud” unambiguously consumes both electricity and water on a scale that is no longer hypothetical.

**Minerals and material.** The semiconductors in a modern server are a distillation of a global supply chain. Silicon wafers come from a few plants in Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, and the United States. The photolithography equipment used to pattern the chips at 3-nanometer resolution comes from a single Dutch company (ASML). Rare-earth elements and specialty metals — tantalum, cobalt, gallium, germanium, neodymium, lithium — come from mines in the Democratic Republic of Congo, Chile, China, Australia, Indonesia, and elsewhere, under working conditions that range from well-regulated to exploitative to dependent on child labor. The assembly happens in factories in China, Vietnam, and Malaysia. Each step embeds labor conditions, environmental costs, and geopolitical dependencies that rarely appear in the product’s marketing. The US and the EU have recently passed legislation (the CHIPS Act in the US, the European Chips Act in the EU) partly motivated by how concentrated the supply chain has become in East Asia and how fragile it is to conflict around Taiwan.

**E-waste.** At the other end of the life cycle, discarded computing equipment — laptops, phones, servers, GPUs — goes to e-waste streams that are often exported to informal recycling operations in West Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia. Workers (often including children) extract valuable metals by burning cable insulation and dissolving circuit boards in acid, with predictable health and environmental consequences. The Basel Convention is supposed to restrict the export of hazardous waste from rich to poor countries, but enforcement is patchy and “reuse” loopholes are routinely exploited. The short obsolescence cycle of consumer electronics — driven in part by design choices that make repair and battery replacement difficult — amplifies the problem.

**Carbon footprint of model training.** Several academic studies since 2019 have attempted to quantify the carbon footprint of training and serving large machine-learning models. The numbers vary widely depending on the grid mix, the model size, the number of training runs needed to get the final model, and whether the inference cost over the model’s deployment lifetime is included. A general finding is that inference cost (serving the model to users) eventually dwarfs training cost for widely-deployed models, which shifts the relevant question from “how much did training cost” to “how efficient is the serving pipeline and how much is the model being used.” This is a place where small engineering decisions — batch sizes, caching, model distillation, quantization — have real environmental consequences at scale. The commercial incentives to reduce inference cost are strong, which is why the serving side has received more engineering attention than training efficiency; but the framing of “efficiency” as an engineering goal rather than an environmental one is itself a political choice about which costs get counted.

The habit to develop here is the same one from every other section of this chapter: *ask where the externalities live*. When you use a cloud service, you are outsourcing the electricity, the water, the minerals, the e-waste, and the labor of the infrastructure to somewhere else. That outsourcing is not automatically bad — specialization and centralization can be genuinely more efficient than running your own server room — but it becomes bad when it lets you stop thinking about the costs. The goal is not to stop using the cloud. The goal is to know what the cloud is made of.

## 8.11 Key laws and policies that shaped the web

Computing does not exist outside law. Every platform, every protocol, every cryptographic choice, every data flow is constrained by a handful of statutes whose history is worth knowing even if you never plan to become a lawyer. This section walks through the most consequential ones for an ordinary developer working in a US or EU context. It is not legal advice, and the law changes; the goal is to give you enough of the shape that you know what to read more about when a question actually comes up.

### The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA, United States, 1986)

The CFAA is the primary US federal statute on computer crime. Originally passed in 1984 and substantially revised in 1986, it makes it a crime to “access a computer without authorization” or to “exceed authorized access.” The ambiguity of those phrases — what counts as “access,” what counts as “without authorization” — has been the subject of three decades of litigation, and the law has been applied to activities as different as breaking into a bank’s servers and scraping publicly-visible web pages.

The most famous CFAA case is the prosecution of **Aaron Swartz**, a co-founder of the RSS standard, Creative Commons, and Reddit. In 2010-2011 Swartz used MIT’s network to download millions of academic articles from the JSTOR database. He was arrested, charged under the CFAA with multiple felony counts carrying a potential decades-long sentence, and — while the case was still pending — took his own life in January 2013 at the age of 26. JSTOR had declined to pursue charges; MIT took a more ambiguous position; the federal prosecutor continued. Swartz’s death prompted a sustained reform campaign (often called “Aaron’s Law”) that proposed narrowing the CFAA’s definition of unauthorized access, but as of this writing the statute remains largely unchanged. The Supreme Court’s 2021 *Van Buren v. United States* decision did narrow the “exceeds authorized access” clause somewhat, holding that it does not apply to people who have legitimate access to a system but use it for a purpose the system’s owner disapproves of. The 2022 *hiQ Labs v. LinkedIn* case further narrowed the “without authorization” clause for scraping of publicly-accessible data. The CFAA is still the law under which most US-based security researchers worry about legal exposure when they find and disclose vulnerabilities.

The practical upshot for you: if you are doing scraping, security research, or any activity that involves poking at a system that is not yours, understand that the CFAA exists and that it has been interpreted unpredictably. “The data was public” is a better defense after *hiQ* than it was before, but it is not yet a blanket one, and prosecutorial discretion is a real factor.

### Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act (United States, 1996)

Section 230 is 26 words long:

> No provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider.

Those 26 words are the legal foundation of the modern social internet. They mean that if you run a platform — a forum, a social network, a review site, a video host, a comment section — you are not legally liable for what your users post, the way a newspaper publisher would be liable for what it prints. They also mean that you are free to moderate user content (remove it, down-rank it, fact-check it) without becoming liable for the content you leave up, which before 1996 was a genuine legal risk: the 1995 *Stratton Oakmont v. Prodigy* case had held that a service that moderated *any* content was a “publisher” of *all* content, which created an absurd incentive to moderate nothing.

Section 230 is why Wikipedia, YouTube, Reddit, Yelp, Stack Overflow, and every comment section can exist in their current form. It is also why every large platform’s moderation decisions are simultaneously important and under-accountable: the platforms make rules that function like speech law for the hundreds of millions of people who depend on them, and they make those rules without democratic process or, usually, meaningful appeal. Calls to reform Section 230 have come from both the political right (who argue that platforms moderate too aggressively) and the political left (who argue that platforms tolerate too much harmful content), and both sides misunderstand what Section 230 actually does in ways worth disentangling before you have the argument. Jeff Kosseff’s book *The Twenty-Six Words That Created the Internet* is the canonical legal history.

### The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA, United States, 1998)

The DMCA has two halves that matter for developers. The first is the **notice-and-takedown system** under Section 512, which gives online service providers a “safe harbor” from copyright liability if they promptly remove material in response to a takedown notice from the copyright holder. This is the legal machinery that makes YouTube’s automated Content ID system possible (and necessary), and it is also the machinery that gets widely abused — fraudulent takedown notices are common, counter-notices are underused, and the 10-14-day window between takedown and restoration is often decisive for time-sensitive content such as political speech during an election.

The second half is Section 1201, the **anti-circumvention provision**, which makes it illegal to “circumvent a technological measure that effectively controls access to a \[copyrighted\] work.” This provision was pitched as an anti-piracy measure, but its application has been much broader: it is the legal instrument that manufacturers use to stop third parties from repairing devices, jailbreaking phones, modifying game consoles, and analyzing the software running in cars and medical equipment. Every three years, the US Copyright Office grants temporary exemptions to Section 1201 for specific activities (security research on medical devices, jailbreaking phones for interoperability, circumvention for educational use of film clips), but the default is restrictive. If you want to know why you cannot run alternative firmware on your smart thermostat, Section 1201 is why.

### Health and education privacy: HIPAA and FERPA (United States)

**HIPAA** (the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, 1996) regulates the handling of protected health information by “covered entities” (healthcare providers, insurers, clearinghouses) and their business associates. For a developer, the practical implication is that if you are building anything that touches identifiable health data, you are in HIPAA’s world and you need a lawyer and an information-security program. HIPAA does not apply to most consumer health apps — your fitness tracker, your period-tracking app, your meditation app — which is why those apps’ privacy practices vary wildly and which is why the US has a persistent political debate about extending HIPAA-like protections to consumer health data.

**FERPA** (the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act, 1974) regulates the handling of student education records by schools and colleges that receive federal funding. For a developer working on ed-tech, FERPA imposes constraints on what data can be shared with third parties, what consents are required, and what rights students and parents have to access and correct records. As with HIPAA, the scope of FERPA coverage is narrower than people often assume — many “education-adjacent” services escape FERPA because they do not meet the technical definition of an education record held by a covered institution.

### The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, European Union, 2018)

The GDPR is the most important piece of privacy legislation in the world, not because it is perfect — it is not — but because it is enforceable, has been enforced, and has shaped the global default for how web services handle personal data. The core ideas:

- **Lawful basis for processing.** You need a specific, documented reason to process someone’s personal data. “Because we have it” is not a reason. The permitted bases are narrow: consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, and “legitimate interest” (which is the catch-all, and the one most contested).
- **Data subject rights.** People whose data you hold have the right to ask what you have on them, get a copy of it in a portable format, correct it, delete it, and object to automated decision-making about them.
- **Data minimization and purpose limitation.** You should collect only what you need, keep it only as long as you need it, and not use it for purposes the subject did not consent to.
- **Breach notification.** If you lose control of personal data, you have to tell the supervisory authority within 72 hours and, in serious cases, the affected individuals.
- **Fines.** Up to 4% of global annual revenue or 20 million euros, whichever is higher.

The GDPR applies extraterritorially: if you are processing personal data of people in the EU, you are in GDPR’s scope regardless of where your company is headquartered. This is why every US website suddenly grew a cookie-consent banner in 2018 and why US companies now routinely run GDPR-compliant data pipelines for non-EU users as well. The **California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA, 2018)** and its successor, the **California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA, 2020)**, extended a subset of the GDPR’s ideas to California residents. Several other US states have since passed their own privacy statutes, producing a patchwork that is difficult to comply with but probably will eventually converge toward something like a US federal privacy law.

### The EU AI Act (European Union, 2024)

The EU AI Act, adopted in 2024, is the first comprehensive regulatory framework for artificial intelligence in a major jurisdiction. It takes a risk-based approach: AI systems are sorted into “unacceptable risk” (banned outright — social scoring, certain kinds of biometric surveillance), “high risk” (subject to heavy compliance requirements — education, employment, credit, law enforcement, critical infrastructure), “limited risk” (disclosure requirements — chatbots, deepfakes), and “minimal risk” (no specific obligations). Foundation models (general-purpose AI) are covered under a separate regime with transparency, safety, and technical-documentation requirements that scale with the compute used to train the model. The Act phases in over several years and its implementation details will be contested in court and in secondary legislation for years to come. As with GDPR, its extraterritorial reach will likely make it the global default.

### Non-US policy that matters

A non-exhaustive list of policies outside the US that shape global computing in 2026:

- **China’s 2017 Cybersecurity Law**, **2021 Data Security Law**, and **2021 Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL)** together establish a data-localization regime, a tiered classification of data sensitivity, and content and security review requirements that apply to any company operating in or collecting data about China.
- **Russia’s SORM regime** and **2019 “sovereign internet” law**, described earlier, require domestic routing and give the state broad surveillance access.
- **India’s IT Rules (2021)** impose content-takedown timelines on platforms, require designated grievance officers, and were amended in 2023 to give a government fact-check unit authority to flag online content as false (a provision later struck down by an Indian high court in 2024).
- **India’s Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023)** is India’s GDPR analog, with some important differences (a narrower set of data-subject rights, a weaker independent regulator).
- **Brazil’s Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados (LGPD, 2020)** is another GDPR-inspired privacy regime.
- **The UK’s Online Safety Act (2023)** imposes a duty of care on platforms to mitigate harm from user content, with a particular focus on child safety, and gives the regulator Ofcom broad enforcement powers including the ability to require scanning of encrypted messages (a provision still contested).

### Net neutrality and common-carrier debates

A recurring policy fight, in the US and elsewhere, is whether internet service providers should be required to carry all traffic without preferential pricing or throttling. The “net neutrality” principle says yes; the anti-net-neutrality position says that ISPs should be free to prioritize traffic and charge content providers for fast lanes. The US has gone back and forth: the FCC classified broadband as a “Title II” common carrier in 2015, reclassified it in 2017, and re-classified it again in 2024. The EU adopted net-neutrality regulations in 2015. The practical effect of net neutrality on the shape of the internet is real but subtle: it affects whether a startup video service can compete with an incumbent on equal terms, whether a poor user’s free mobile plan can include unlimited Facebook but not Wikipedia, and whether an ISP can throttle a service its own parent company competes with.

### What to take away

You do not need to memorize statutes. You need to know that:

1.  Your day-to-day engineering decisions are shaped by laws that were passed in specific years, for specific reasons, and could have been passed differently. The defaults of modern web development — takedown notices, cookie banners, consent flows, content moderation rules — all trace to specific legal texts with specific histories.
2.  The laws governing computing are more jurisdictionally fragmented than the computing itself. A single app may have to comply with GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA, COPPA, the DMCA, the EU AI Act, India’s DPDP Act, and a handful of others at the same time.
3.  When a product’s behavior does not match what you think “should” be possible — you cannot modify your thermostat, you cannot export your data, you cannot scrape a public page, a search result disappears for EU users — there is often a specific statute responsible, and looking it up is a normal part of being a literate practitioner.
4.  The law is not the ceiling of what you can do; it is the floor. Ethical practice usually asks more of you than statutes do, and many of the most important design decisions are made in the gap between “legal” and “right.”

## 8.12 Worked examples

### Example 1: reading Robert Moses’s overpasses the way Winner did

Winner opens his essay with the story of the low overpasses on Long Island’s parkways, and it is worth slowing down to practice the analytical move he is making. The overpasses are a concrete, physical object. They have a stated technical purpose (they carry a road over another road) and a stated design constraint (they are low). The “politics” of the overpasses is not in the bricks; it is in what that low clearance *does* in a social context where buses are taller than private cars and bus riders are disproportionately poor and non-white. The bricks perform the social sorting because the other options — a zoning policy, a beach-access fee, a police deployment — would have been politically costly in ways the bricks were not.

The move to practice is: for any artifact you use, (1) state its technical function plainly, (2) ask what social arrangement its shape makes easier and what it makes harder, (3) ask whether that arrangement is an accident of history, a deliberate choice by an identifiable decision-maker, or an emergent consequence of economic pressure on the designer. For a software example, consider **captchas**. Stated technical function: distinguish humans from bots. Effect on social arrangement: makes web access harder for people with visual impairments, people on slow connections, and people whose native scripts are not well represented in the training data for the challenge. Who decided? No single person; the decision is distributed across hundreds of product managers making a cost-benefit trade-off between “fraud” and “accessibility” where the second cost is borne by people the product manager will never meet. That distributed decision is what political philosophers call *structural*. The overpasses had a single architect; captchas have no architect, but the politics are equally legible once you name them.

### Example 2: the Aaron Swartz case and what the CFAA does when you are not looking

Aaron Swartz was 26 when he died in January 2013. He had already co-authored the RSS 1.0 specification as a teenager, co-founded Creative Commons, built part of the early Reddit, and was an active participant in the campaign that defeated the Stop Online Piracy Act (SOPA) in 2011-2012. He was also, in the eyes of the US Attorney’s Office for the District of Massachusetts, a federal criminal: he had used MIT’s network to download a large fraction of the JSTOR academic-article database in 2010-2011, and he had been arrested for it and charged with multiple CFAA and wire-fraud counts.

The case is worth studying because almost every feature of it is a policy choice rather than an inevitability. JSTOR, the nominal victim, recovered the downloaded files and declined to pursue civil or criminal action. MIT, whose network Swartz used, chose not to take a position for or against prosecution — a decision MIT’s own internal review later called out as unusual given the university’s historical stance on open access. The federal prosecutor chose to press forward with a multi-count indictment carrying a maximum of decades in prison, even though the likely actual sentence was much shorter. Swartz chose to fight rather than accept a plea deal that would have required him to accept a felony conviction, because he believed the prosecution was wrong on principle. The family’s statement after his death argued that the weight of those combined choices — the prosecutor’s, MIT’s, the law’s — was a decisive contributing factor to his suicide.

For you, as a student, there are three portable lessons. First, the laws you never studied in a computing class may matter more to your working life than some of the ones you did. Second, “access” is a legal concept before it is a technical one, and the gap between “the system let me do this” and “I had authorization to do this” is where most legal trouble in computing lives. Third, the people who push the boundaries of what computing *could* be are sometimes the people the existing system punishes hardest. That does not mean you should break the law, but it does mean you should know the law well enough to know what you are choosing when you do not.

### Example 3: Gender Shades and what measurement can do

Joy Buolamwini was a graduate student at the MIT Media Lab when she noticed that a face-tracking library she was using for an art project did not detect her face until she put on a white mask. That experience led her, with Timnit Gebru, to build the first balanced benchmark for face-analysis accuracy across skin type and gender, and to use that benchmark to evaluate three commercial APIs. The 2018 “Gender Shades” paper ([Buolamwini and Gebru 2018](#ref-buolamwini2018gendershades)) was the result: a clean, replicable, measured demonstration that the industry’s best commercial face-analysis systems worked dramatically worse on darker-skinned women than on lighter-skinned men.

The response from the vendors was not uniform. IBM issued a statement within 24 hours of the paper’s release and released a new version with substantially reduced disparities. Microsoft did similarly. Face++ was slower to respond publicly. A follow-up paper in 2019 (“Actionable Auditing”) re-evaluated the updated systems and found that the disparities had narrowed but not closed, and that one new vendor (Amazon Rekognition) still had large disparities. Amazon’s public response was, initially, to dispute the methodology.

The portable lesson here is that measurement is a political act. Before Gender Shades, nobody at the vendor companies had a clean, public number for their systems’ disparate accuracy, which meant that nobody inside or outside the companies could argue over how large the problem was. After Gender Shades, the number existed, which meant that (1) the disparities became a KPI that executives could be held to, (2) external researchers could run the same evaluation on new systems and compare, and (3) the political conversation about face-recognition shifted from “does bias exist” to “what is the acceptable level and who decides.” The number did not fix the problem. It made the problem fixable. That is often the best thing an individual researcher can do, and it is a reason to take measurement seriously as a civic practice and not just a technical one.

## 8.13 Exercises

1.  **Apply Winner’s thesis to a tool you use daily.** Pick one piece of software you use every day — a messaging app, an IDE, a payment app, a text editor, a search engine, an AI chatbot. For each, say whether you think the arrangement is the result of a deliberate design choice, an emergent economic pressure, or an accident of history. One page.

Write a one-paragraph summary of (a) what the law literally says, (b) how it has been interpreted in practice, and (c) one specific engineering decision you can point to that exists *because* of this law.

Find one of her primary publications or a long-form interview. Write a one-paragraph summary of her argument or contribution, and one paragraph on why you had not heard of her before.

Read whatever documentation exists. Answer: which population is represented, which population is not represented, what language(s) the data is in, who collected it and under what funding, what consent framework (if any) was used, and what uses the dataset was originally designed for versus what it is being used for now. 5.  **Map a platform’s gatekeeping decisions.** Pick one major platform (GitHub, Google Play, Apple App Store, Cloudflare, AWS, YouTube, Twitter/X, Meta). For each, identify whether the decision was driven by a law, a company policy, a commercial relationship, public pressure, or a combination. What does the pattern tell you about where the power actually sits?

6.  **Measure your own stack’s environmental footprint.** Pick one cloud service you use (Google Colab, AWS, a hosted database, an API-based LLM). Find the provider’s published sustainability documentation. Calculate or estimate the carbon and water footprint of one month of your usage. Note the assumptions you had to make and the data you could not get. One page.

7.  **Read a historical primary source.** Read either Winner’s 1980 essay ([Winner 1980](#ref-winner1980artifacts)) in full (it is short) or one chapter of one of the books cited in this chapter. Write one paragraph on what the argument got right, one paragraph on what it got wrong, and one paragraph on what it would say about a technology that did not exist at the time of writing.

8.  **Contrast two takes.** Read Morozov’s *The Net Delusion* ([Morozov 2011](#ref-morozov2011net)) and Tufekci’s *Twitter and Tear Gas* ([Tufekci 2017](#ref-tufekci2017twitter)) — or at least their introductions and one chapter each. Write a one-paragraph comparison of how each author thinks about the relationship between networked platforms and democratic politics. Which one do you think is closer to right about the 2020s, and why?

## 8.14 One-page checklist

Before you adopt, advocate for, or build a technology, ask yourself:

- **Origin.** Who built this? Who paid for it? Whose problem were they solving?
- **Values.** What values are encoded in the defaults? (Privacy-by-default? Openness-by-default? Surveillance-by-default?) What would it mean to flip each default?
- **Beneficiaries.** Who benefits most from this working as designed? Who benefits least?
- **Burden.** Who pays the cost when it fails? Who pays the cost when it works “correctly” but hurts someone?
- **Representation.** Whose data is in the training set (if any)? Whose language is the interface in? Whose infrastructure does it assume?
- **Labor.** What human labor is hidden inside the automated parts? Where is that labor, under what conditions, at what wage?
- **Material.** What physical resources does it consume? Where does the electricity come from? Where does the waste go?
- **Power.** Who can change the rules? Who can appeal? What does the appeal process actually look like?
- **Law.** What statutes shape what this thing can and cannot do? What would have to change in the law for this thing to exist differently?
- **History.** What did the world look like before this technology? What got lost and what got gained? Could the losses have been avoided?
- **Alternatives.** What would a version of this tool built around a different set of values look like? Does that version exist? Why doesn’t it win?
- **Your role.** When you use this tool, what are you endorsing? When you build on top of it, what are you perpetuating? When you teach others to use it, what are you normalizing?

You will not have good answers to all of these for any given technology. The discipline is in *asking* them, and in noticing the ones you cannot answer, because those are the places where the politics of the artifact are hiding in plain sight.
